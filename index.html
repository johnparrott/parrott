Course Welcome Video Demo

Every course has an opening scene.

Every course has an opening scene. Before the schedule, assignments, and expectations come into focus, students begin to feel what the course is about and whether there is something there worth entering into.

I created this welcome video for a fictional version of Developmental Psychology Across the Lifespan as a way of designing that opening scene. I thought of it as a course trailer, using pacing, visuals, music, and narration to introduce the emotional shape of the class.

The video invites students into developmental psychology as a story of human growth: how people change across time, how lives unfold, and how each of us becomes who we are. I wanted the first encounter with the course to feel coherent, welcoming, and meaningful.

Syllabus Demo

A syllabus is more than a set of rules.

A syllabus is often the first document students receive, and it can quietly shape how a student understands the course. It can feel like a contract, a checklist, or a wall of requirements. I wanted this one to feel like a guide.

I created this syllabus for a fictional version of Developmental Psychology Across the Lifespan as a way of imagining how students might be oriented into the course with clarity and care. The structure follows the questions students often bring at the beginning of a semester: What is this class about? What will I learn? What will I do? How will I be supported? How will I know what is expected of me?

The goal was to show students the path ahead while also communicating that they were entering a learning environment built with intention, support, and curiosity.

Asynchronous Lecture Demo

Meaning-making, made experiential.

An asynchronous lecture can easily become something students simply watch. Because this lecture was about meaning-making, I wanted the structure of the lecture to do more than explain the concept. I wanted students to experience it as they moved through the lesson.

I created this lecture demo for a fictional version of Developmental Psychology Across the Lifespan to introduce meaning-making as both a concept and an experience. Students begin by reflecting on what meaning means to them, then move into a connect-the-dots activity that makes the process of meaning-making visible.

That activity becomes the bridge into the theory. Students see how scattered experiences, expectations, and interpretations can form patterns that help us navigate the world. By returning to the opening reflection at the end, the lecture models the process it teaches: beginning with intuition, encountering new ideas, making connections, and coming back with a changed perspective.

Adult Development Museum Activity · PSYCH 330

A gallery for the story of adulthood.

At the end of a course, I want students to do more than remember what they covered. I want them to see what the course has become in their own thinking.

For a final wrap-up activity as a TA in Adult Development, I asked students to imagine they were designing a museum exhibit about adulthood. Their task was to create a visible, spatial story of emerging, middle, and later adulthood: how these periods connect, how they differ, and what assumptions about adult life might need to be challenged.

The museum format gave students a way to synthesize the course creatively and collaboratively. They had to decide what mattered, how ideas fit together, and how to communicate those ideas to a specific audience. The activity turned review into design, and design into a way of making meaning.
